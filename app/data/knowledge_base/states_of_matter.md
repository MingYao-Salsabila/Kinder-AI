# States of Matter

Everything around us is made of matter, and matter usually exists in one of three states: solid, liquid, or gas. The state of matter depends on how the tiny particles inside it are arranged and how much energy they have.

A solid has a fixed shape and volume. Its particles are packed closely together in a regular pattern and barely move. Ice, wood, and rocks are all solids.

A liquid has a fixed volume but no fixed shape -- it takes the shape of whatever container it is in. Its particles are close together but can slide past each other, so liquids can flow. Water, milk, and juice are all liquids.

A gas has no fixed shape or volume. Its particles are spread far apart and move quickly in every direction, so a gas spreads out to fill its container. The air we breathe is a mixture of gases.

Matter can change from one state to another when it is heated or cooled. When a solid is heated enough, it melts into a liquid. When a liquid is heated enough, it evaporates, or boils, into a gas. When a gas is cooled, it can condense into a liquid, and when a liquid is cooled enough, it can freeze into a solid.

Water is a great example because we can easily see all three states: ice (solid), water (liquid), and steam (gas).

## Key vocabulary
- Solid: matter with a fixed shape and volume, like ice or wood.
- Liquid: matter with a fixed volume but no fixed shape, like water.
- Gas: matter with no fixed shape or volume that spreads out to fill its container, like air.
- Melting, freezing, evaporating, and condensing: the changes between states of matter caused by heating or cooling.
